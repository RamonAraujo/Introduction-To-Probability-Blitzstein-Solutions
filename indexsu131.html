To prove pairwise independence: Let J, J' be two arbitrary subsets of {1...n}. We want to show that P(YJ=a∧YJ'=b)=P(YJ=a)P(YJ=b)=1∕4 for a,b∈{0,1}, with the second equality coming from our knowledge that YJ∼Bern(1∕2) for all J.

First, let us note that for all J,J' that are disjoint, YJ,YJ' are independent - this follows from the independence of the Xi.

Now, suppose J,J' are not disjoint. Let A=J∩J', let B=J∖A, and let C=J'∖B. By definition, A,B,C are disjoint.

Now, we have

P(YJ=a∧YJ'=b)=P(YJ=a,YJ'=b|YA=1)P(YA=1)+P(YJ=a,YJ'=b|YA=0)P(YA=0)

using the LOTP. Continuing, we have

=P(YB=1−a,YC=1−b|YA=1)P(YA=1)+P(YB=a,YC=b|YA=0)P(YA=1)

by noting that if x⊕1=y, we must have y=1−x and if x⊕1=y, we have y=x. Continuing, we get

=P(YB=1−a)P(YC=1−b)P(YA=1)+P(YB=a)P(YC=b)P(YA=0)

We can remove the conditioning since A,B,C are disjoint, and therefore YB,YC,YA are all independent r.v.s. Finally, we realize that since all Y are Bern(1∕2), this results in

=(1∕2)3+(1∕2)3=1∕4=P(YJ=a)P(YJ'=b)

as desired - YJ,YJ' are independent for any pair J,J'.

To prove that the YJ are not all independent, consider the subsets S={1},S'={2},S''{1,2}. It is clear that if YS=1 and YS'=1, then YS''=YS⊕YS'=0. However, this implies that

P(⋂J⊆{1..n}YJ=1)=0

i.e. it is impossible for all Y to simultaneously equal 1. However, we know that

∏J⊆{1..n}P(YJ=1)=(1∕2)2n−1≠0

Thus, the YJ are not independent.
